SimpleX Chat supports end-to-end encrypted audio and video calls using **WebRTC**. All call signaling — the exchange of session descriptions, ICE candidates, and cryptographic keys — flows through your existing encrypted SimpleX messaging channel rather than through a dedicated signaling server. This means that even the infrastructure facilitating the call never sees its contents.

<Note>
  Group calls are not currently supported. Calls are available between two contacts only.
</Note>

## Starting a call

**Audio call:**
1. Open a conversation with a contact.
2. Tap the **phone icon** (📞) to the right of the contact's name at the top of the screen.

**Video call:**
1. Open a conversation with a contact.
2. Tap the **three vertical dots** (⋮) in the top-right corner.
3. Choose **Video call**.

The recipient will see an incoming call notification and can **accept**, **reject** (silently, without notifying you), or **ignore** it (to accept later while you are still waiting). There is no timeout — the call invitation stays active as long as you remain in the call screen.

## How call signaling works

WebRTC requires an initial exchange of **SDP offer/answer** packets and **ICE candidates** between the two peers before the media stream is established. In most apps this signaling passes through a dedicated server, which can observe call metadata.

In SimpleX:

1. When you initiate a call, the SDP offer is **encrypted and sent as a regular SimpleX message** through your existing contact connection.
2. Your contact's device decrypts it, generates an SDP answer, and sends it back through the same encrypted channel.
3. ICE candidates are exchanged the same way.
4. Once both peers have each other's ICE information the WebRTC media stream is established directly (peer-to-peer or via TURN relay).

Because the STUN/TURN server only sees encrypted WebRTC traffic after the signaling phase, it cannot read call content. The relay server also never receives signaling packets.

## STUN and TURN servers

WebRTC uses two types of relay infrastructure for NAT traversal:

| Server type | Role | IP visibility |
|-------------|------|---------------|
| **STUN** | Discovers your public IP so peers can connect directly | Your IP is visible to your contact |
| **TURN** | Relays media packets when direct P2P fails | Your IP is hidden from your contact |

SimpleX provides preset STUN/TURN servers (`stun.simplex.im` / `turn.simplex.im`) used by default. These servers can observe the **duration** of your calls but not the content.

You can switch between relay and peer-to-peer modes:

- **Always use relay** (default, on): media goes through TURN — your IP is hidden from your contact.
- **Always use relay** (off): SimpleX attempts a direct P2P connection — your IP will be visible to your contact, but the preset TURN server cannot observe the call duration.

## Setting a custom TURN server

To use your own WebRTC ICE server:

1. Open **Settings → Audio & Video calls → WebRTC ICE servers**.
2. Toggle **Configure ICE servers** on.
3. Enter your server addresses, one per line, in the format:

```text
turn:user:pass@turn.example.com:3478
```

4. Tap **Save**.

<Info>
  For maximum call privacy, use a self-hosted TURN server with TLS. See the [WebRTC TURN server setup guide](/server/webrtc-turn) for configuration instructions.
</Info>

<Note>
  WebRTC ICE server settings are stored on the current device only, not in the chat database. If you migrate your database to a new device you must reconfigure these settings manually.
</Note>

## Privacy considerations

| Scenario | Your IP visible to contact? | Your IP visible to TURN server? |
|----------|----------------------------|---------------------------------|
| Default (preset TURN, relay on) | No | Yes (call duration only) |
| Relay off (P2P) | Yes | No |
| Self-hosted TURN | No | Only you control the server |
| Self-hosted TURN + Tor (Android/Orbot) | No | Tor exit node IP only |

- **With default settings**, your IP is hidden from your contact but the preset TURN server can log call duration.
- **For maximum IP privacy**, run your own TURN server and route the connection through Tor via Orbot (see [Privacy Settings](/guide/privacy-settings)).
